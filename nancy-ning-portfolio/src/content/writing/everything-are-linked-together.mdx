There is a particular feeling that comes when you understand something deeply enough that you start to see its shape everywhere else.

It happened to me first with mathematics. Once you understand how a proof works — how you build from axioms to conclusion without gaps — you begin to notice the same structure in good arguments, in well-designed software, in the way a strong essay moves.

## Everything connects

Systems thinkers like to say that you cannot change just one thing. Every element of a system is connected to every other element, and the connections are often more important than the elements themselves.

This is uncomfortable for people who like clean solutions. It means that fixing one problem often creates another. It means that the most important leverage points in a system are often counter-intuitive — they're not where the pressure is most visible.

## What this means practically

If you want to understand something complex, don't start by breaking it into parts. Start by finding the relationships. Ask: what does this depend on? What depends on this? What would happen if this were removed?

This is not mysticism. It's just taking seriously the idea that context matters — that things mean different things in different systems, and that you can't understand the part without understanding the whole.

The corollary: if you want to change something, don't attack it directly. Change the system it lives in.
